# Ideas for Training for Sport Climbing

## Core Principles
- Most training should be climbing-specific (wall/rock), with supplemental directed work as needed.
- Variety matters: change venues, structures, and exercises across cycles.
- Individual response varies, so use trial-and-error and a training log.

## Energy System Model
- Anaerobic alactic: very high output, very short duration.
- Anaerobic lactic: high output, lactate-producing, sustained hard efforts.
- Aerobic: lower output, long-duration support and recovery.

Training components used in this framework:
- Aerobic Capacity (`Aero Cap`)
- Anaerobic Capacity (`An Cap`)
- Aerobic Power (`Aero Pow`)
- Anaerobic Power (`An Pow`)
- Strength

## What Each Component Does
- `Aero Cap`: raises sustainable effort and improves recovery at rests.
- `An Cap`: improves ability to execute longer hard sequences.
- `Aero Pow`: improves performance under high pump/endurance stress.
- `An Pow`: improves short, intense power-endurance bursts.
- `Strength`: supports all route types and usually needs regular emphasis.

## Capacity vs Power (Practical)
- Capacity: how much work the system can sustain.
- Power: how hard the system can output.
- Route performance needs both, with emphasis based on route style and goals.

## Periodization Approach
- `Base phase`: prioritize Strength + `An Cap` + `Aero Cap`; maintain powers.
- `Peak phase`: prioritize `Aero Pow` + `An Pow`; maintain strength; increase route specificity.
- Late peak: include mixed circuits that replicate objective structure (hard sections, rests, pacing).

## Adaptation Timelines (Approx.)
- `Aero Cap`: 8+ weeks
- `An Cap`: 16+ weeks
- `Aero Pow` + `An Pow`: 6-8 weeks

## Example Cycle Logic
- Longer base, shorter peak (for example, ~16 weeks base and ~8 weeks peak) when time allows.
- If short on time, compress phases based on weaknesses and objective demands.

## Weekly Loading Guidelines
- Hard system sessions (`An Cap`, `An Pow`, `Aero Pow`): usually up to 2-3 per week depending on tolerance.
- `An Cap`: commonly 1-2 sessions/week.
- `Aero Pow`: commonly ~2 sessions/week when prioritized.
- Keep enough `Aero Cap` while building `An Cap` to handle lactate load.

## Taper Guidance (Pre-Trip / Pre-Performance)
- 1-2 weeks taper.
- Reduce volume to about half while keeping intensity high.
- Drop high-volume base work (`An Cap`, `Aero Cap`, ARC) late in taper.
- Keep hard, high-quality climbing efforts and specificity work.

## Ordering Rule When Combining Work
Use highest intensity first:
- Bouldering -> `An Cap`/`An Pow` -> `Aero Pow` -> `Aero Cap` -> ARC

## Training Methods by Component (Quick)
- `Aero Cap`: sustained controllable pump, longer work intervals, no terminal failure.
- `An Cap`: 12-15 move hard circuits/boulders with longer rests.
- `Aero Pow`: sustained hard pump circuits with shorter rests.
- `An Pow`: short hard boulders with very short rests.

## Key Implementation Notes
- Match training to objective demands (length, rests, intensity profile, crux placement).
- Progress by adjusting difficulty/volume/rest while protecting quality.
- Keep records and review response weekly.

# Ideas for Training for Bouldering

## Programming Structure Ideas
- Use four training categories across the week:
  - Hands (hangboard/campus)
  - Climbing
  - Strength/Power (pulling muscles, lock-offs, explosive pulling)
  - Conditioning (deadlift, bench press, shoulder press, row)
- Keep warm-up and cool-down in every session.
- Try to leave at least one day between high hand/finger loading sessions.

## Cycle and Recovery Ideas
- 6-12 week training cycles are workable.
- Consider a deload/easy week after around 5 hard weeks.
- Around week 10, consider 2-4 weeks of mostly climbing (reduced structured loading) to avoid overuse and keep transfer high.

## Climbing Activities
- Limit Bouldering:
  - Work problems at or slightly above limit.
  - Typical short block: 30 minutes, 2-3 hard problems.
  - Full rest between attempts.
- Board climbing:
  - Use systems boards for repeatable limit efforts and benchmarking.
  - Select problems tied to goal style and current weaknesses.
  - Keep rest long enough to preserve high-quality attempts.
- Volume day:
  - High number of total climbs with generous rest between efforts.
  - Use variable wall angles and stay around flash grade.

## Climbing Session Rules (Tight Summary)
- Rule 1: Climb on the intended holds.
  - Do not bypass movement by grabbing footholds or gaming sequences in training.
- Rule 2: Learn each move in multiple styles.
  - Practice both static and dynamic solutions when possible.
- Rule 3: Do not over-crimp.
  - Use grip types intentionally; reserve full crimp for when it is actually needed.
- Rule 4: Keep one "impossible" move/project in rotation.
  - Revisit over months/years to build long-term movement and confidence.
- Rule 5: Remove options to force adaptation.
  - Example constraints: no thumbs, no heel hooks, no matching.
- Rule 6: No complaining, solve the puzzle.
  - Treat body-size/style constraints as problem-solving inputs, not excuses.
- Rule 7: Performance mode is different.
  - In practice, prioritize learning; in performance, use any legal method to send.

## Power-Endurance Activities
- Boulder 4x4s:
  - 4 problems (below max), minimal rest between problems, then 4 minutes rest.
  - 4 total rounds.
- Boulder link-ups:
  - Link multiple easier problems without touching ground.
  - Repeat to failure, short rest, then repeat.

## Technique Drills
- Precise climbing drill:
  - Climb easier problems with precise feet and precise hand placements, with no unnecessary readjustments.
- Static vs dynamic drill:
  - Climb same problem once as static as possible, then again using momentum.
- One-leg drill:
  - Climb with one leg only, then repeat with the other leg.
- Hover drill and hang-arounds:
  - Use as movement control drills during climbing blocks.

## Hands and Finger Activities
- Hangboard repeaters:
  - Multiple short hangs with brief rests; useful for forearm endurance.
- Max hangs:
  - Short maximal hangs on selected edge sizes.

## Campus
- Campus boulders
- Campus board ladders (minimal volume)

## Pulling Strength and Power Activities
- Weighted pull-ups
- Muscle-ups
- One-arm pull-up progressions

## Conditioning
- Deadlift
- Bench press
- Shoulder press
- Row
- Floor core

## Progression Ideas
- Progress by increasing one variable at a time:
  - difficulty
  - density (less rest)
  - total quality volume
  - external load
- For power and max-strength work, keep quality high and avoid grinding fatigue.
- For endurance-focused blocks, increase repeat quality/consistency before adding major intensity.

# How to Hangboarding

## Role of Hangboarding
- Hangboarding is supplemental training, not a replacement for climbing.
- Primary value: isolated, measurable, and repeatable finger-strength loading.
- Goal is climbing performance, not hangboard numbers.

## Core Principles
- Keep climbing as the priority while hangboarding.
- Avoid protocol-hopping; stay on a method long enough to adapt.
- Keep sessions short and high quality.
- Warm up thoroughly, then hangboard before heavy fatigue.
- Train mostly open crimp + half crimp; add special grips only when goal-specific.
- Use a consistent edge (typically ~15-20mm) and progress load first.

## Who Should Use It
- Beginner:
  - Prioritize climbing and movement learning.
  - Use light/simple hangboarding only as needed for consistency/resilience.
  - Avoid high-volume regimens.
- Intermediate to Advanced:
  - 1-2 focused sessions every 5-10 days can drive meaningful gains.
  - Common session flow: warm-up climbs -> hangboard -> near-limit climbing -> minimal accessories.
- Advanced to Elite:
  - More precise control of load, recovery, and timing.
  - Quality sessions matter more than arbitrary frequency.

## Phase Focus
- Structural-focused phases:
  - Higher volume, lower relative load.
  - Useful for tissue capacity and base building.
- Neuromuscular-focused phases:
  - Lower volume, higher load.
  - Useful near performance periods.
- Keep climbing during all phases; "rest phase" means reduced load, not zero climbing.

## Recovery Rules
- Prioritize sleep, food, and hydration first.
- If session quality drops repeatedly, adjust work:recovery.
- Prefer resting for a quality session over forcing low-quality sessions.

## Recommended Grip and Setup Notes
- Primary grips: open crimp, half crimp.
- Full crimp: optional, conservative volume/intensity only.
- Pockets/pinches/slopers: use mostly when goal-specific.
- Keep shoulders active; no passive hanging.

## Hangboard Protocols

### 6:10 Repeaters (Workhorse)
- 6s on : 10s off x 5 reps
- Rest 2-3 min between sets
- 4-6 total sets
- Goal: finish near failure on rep 5 without form breakdown

### 6:6x6+ (Structural/Work Capacity)
- 6s on : 6s off x 6+ reps to controlled form failure
- Rest ~2 min between sets
- 4-6 total sets
- Progress by raising load once you hit top of target rep range

### Single Hangs (Neuromuscular Focus)
- Single hang ~4-8s (6s common)
- Rest 2-4 min
- 4-8 total hangs
- Use high load; avoid excessive volume

### Long Hangs (Structural / Tissue Resilience)
- 15-30s hangs to controlled failure
- Rest 2-4 min
- 3-5 total hangs
- Best used farther from peak performance blocks

## Practical Scheduling Notes
- Can hangboard and climb on the same day if quality/recovery are managed.
- Match climbing stimulus around hangboard stress.
- Log sessions and adjust based on performance trends and finger/shoulder status.

# Foundations of training for climbing

## Core Framing
- There is no single best training method for all climbers.
- Practice can be ahead of research, but decisions should still be grounded in physiology and critical thinking.
- Individual context matters: goals, time budget, fear/anxiety profile, training age, climbing age, and recovery capacity.
- Good planning prioritizes the constraint that most limits real performance, not just what is easiest to measure.

## Decision-Making Principle
- If two options both improve performance, prioritize the one that removes the bigger bottleneck.
- Example: if fear of falling prevents full effort, reducing fear may unlock more progress than adding strength work.

## 7 Foundations

### 1) Climbing is highly variable
- Different disciplines and styles demand different technical, physical, and mental qualities.
- Training should be specific to both the person and the target style/objective.

### 2) Climbing stresses delicate structures
- Hands and shoulders are exposed to high repetitive loads.
- Injury risk rises when load exceeds preparedness.
- Build resilience with appropriate progressive strength work and adequate time to adapt.

### 3) Climbing is skill-dominant
- Skill acquisition requires broad movement exposure across styles/terrain.
- Continue refining efficiency and movement quality at all levels.

### 4) Physical attributes still matter
- Strength, power, and endurance are necessary to express skill.
- Do not over-prioritize measurable metrics at the expense of movement skill.
- Physical development should support both performance and injury resilience.

### 5) Ability requires time and energy
- Progress depends on both elapsed time and quality practice volume.
- Improvement rate slows with advancement; this is normal.
- Track progress beyond grade-chasing.

### 6) Practice and performance are different
- Practice: deconstruct, train components, reintegrate.
- Performance: apply everything with full commitment.
- Athletes should intentionally switch between both modes.

### 7) Performance is also training stress
- Hard performance efforts (projects, comps) create major physical and mental load.
- Account for this in weekly stress/recovery planning.

## Practical Application Rules
- Build plans from first principles before adding trendy methods.
- Use new methods only when they serve the actual bottleneck.
- Reassess frequently and adapt the plan as constraints change.

# Beginner Guide to Training for Climbing

## First-Year Priority: Skill Acquisition
- Climbing is a skill sport; movement quality and decision-making come first.
- Spend most session time actually climbing.
- Build both volume and style breadth:
  - slab
  - vert
  - overhang
  - roof
  - comp-style movement
- Climb with others to accelerate learning:
  - observe
  - ask questions
  - compare movement options

## Tactics to Build Early
- Focus on how you climb, not only whether you top out.
- Repeat climbs to refine movement and reduce wasted energy.
- Brush holds when friction matters (especially slopers and pinches).
- Rest longer between hard attempts; use a timer.
- Warm up properly:
  - hips/mobility
  - upper-body activation
- Practice route reading and reflection after each attempt.

## Route-Reading Drills
- Second-go redpoint:
  - identify mistakes from attempt one and immediately correct on attempt two.
- Flash/onsight practice:
  - preview, visualize, then execute first try.
- Setter-intent drill:
  - infer why hold orientation and sequencing were set as they were.

## Finger Strength for Beginners
- Step 1: Build strength on the wall first.
  - climbing improves fingers while also improving skill and coordination.
- Step 2: Vary grip types.
  - avoid over-relying on one grip style.
- Step 3: Add hangboarding only when needed.
  - keep it supplemental, not ego-driven.
  - prioritize low-risk loading and capacity development.
  - begin with light/longer hangs (for example ~20s with assistance).
  - avoid max testing and high-intensity protocols early.

## Beginner S&C Priorities
- Purpose:
  - improve tissue resilience
  - reduce injury risk
  - increase ability to tolerate climbing volume
- Start with fundamentals:
  - pull-ups (scaled as needed)
  - press-ups
  - basic rows/pressing accessories if recovery allows
- Add basic floor core:
  - bracing and trunk control work
  - simple progressive variations
- Add deadlifts only with good form and coaching when needed.

## Programming Guidance
- Keep S&C simple and place it after skill-focused climbing work.
- Add only what your recovery can support.
- If added training lowers climbing quality, reduce supplemental load first.
